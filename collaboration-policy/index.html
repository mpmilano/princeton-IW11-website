Collaboration Policy

Note that this policy is still under construction, and as such is subject to change

So, we need to talk about chatGPT. As a practitioner and professor, I find myself really torn about how to manage the challenge of modern generative LLMs in a university setting. On the one hand, I want to acknowledge the utility of chatGPT and copilot to solve previously-intractable problems, and their ubiquity in industry today. On the other hand, we’re not trying to become promp engineers here: we’re trying to become systems engineers.

With that in mind, here is the rough policy on chatGPT, copilot, or any other similar tool:

  • you may not use these tools to generate text for our paper submissions, forms, or other non-code tasks
  • you may not use these tools to generate significant chunks of application logic or assist in algorithmic design
  • you may use these tools to generate API calls, set-up and tear-down of environments, or any other code which you would reasonably expect to find verbatim in the documentation of a tool or in a tutorial on its use.

I’m working with the McGraw center to tighten up these guidelines, but I hope this provides some clarity.

For collaboration with humans

Please, collaborate away! Just remember: your final report must feature independent work, and thus when collaborating you must bear in mind the need to “own” a significant chunk of the final project. Feel free to help each other debug code, enlist friends for support, or otherwise leverage your community when pursuing these projects. The only golden rule is: your final report is your own, describing work that you did during the semester, on a [major component of a] project that you designed.
