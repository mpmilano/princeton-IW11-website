I am writing to welcome you to the Independent Work Seminar on “Wrestling with Distributed Systems" and to introduce myself as your independent work adviser. Welcome!

Almost every major application out there is a distributed system, even things as simple as Microsoft Word or as complicated as the stock market. You interact with distributed systems when you turn on your phone, send an email or message, buy a ticket to a concert, or do almost any basic activity as part of daily life. And while working in core distributed systems can be mountains of complexity, the distributed applications built atop core systems—the actual chat clients, ticket sales, or email portals—are often surprisingly simple. Using cloud-based distributed services, one can build a scalable, responsive distributed chat server—the “hello world” of distributed systems—with as much ease as building the local equivalent just on a laptop.

In this IW seminar, we’ll be using services exposed directly by the major clouds to build distributed applications of your choosing! Along the way we’ll discuss what potential applications you’d be most interested in building, how cloud infrastructure works in practice, how to navigate the choices between many different cloud providers and, ultimately, how to build an application by gluing together these existing services and get a seamless solution as a result.

In order to plan an excellent semester for all of us, it’ll be really helpful to know a little bit more about your backgrounds on day zero—before we’ve even had the first lecture. To collect this information, I’ve made a little google form with questions about what programming languages you’ve used, what parts of computer science excite you, and your comfort on the command line. Please fill it out within the next few days! I’ll use the results to build our first lecture, so the more information I’m able to get from you, the better that lecture will be :)

Students are welcome to work on separate, unrelated projects. However, it is my hope that we’ll develop into a couple small groups which will work on related projects, allowing us to take advantage of shared infrastructure and shared knowledge to greatly enhance each projects' potential! For example, two students could collaborate on one application, with one student handling the traditional “backend” concerns—like handling distributed storage—while another works on “frontend” concerns, like the visible web portal or other application-specific logic. Or some students could work on coding/implementations while another student explores correctness or formalizes security requirements. Of course, collaboration is not required, but I would like to encourage it because I think it will be more fun and productive for everybody.
